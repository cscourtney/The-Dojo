Takedown Reference List (KK Doc)

  • Ankle Pick: Pull head or same-side collar to floor; once you have ankle, shove head to floor and spin.
  • Water Bird
  • Armdrag to Leg Wrap (Lineman)
  • Quarter Nelson: With underhook, L hand to CROWN of head, R hand grabs L wrist to reinforce. Spin to ground straight into armbar.
  • Underhook to water bird fail → consider quarter nelson or foot sweep inside or outside.
  • Arm drag to valley drop
  • Guard pull to leg sweep. Guard pull to ankle pick/pull.
  • Double leg. Front Head Lock. Drinking Bird / Half Nelson / Valley Drop.
  • GI High Elbow Takedown: Same-side sleeve control, elbow inside their arm then high to sky, then forward push takedown.

Armbar from Guard — Wilson

  • Indicator: both arms extended inside. Start R arm to OP L sleeve/wrist; L hand grips OP L lapel.
  • Use legs to break posture. L hand firm trap R tricep and pull L arm across body.
  • R arm lets go sleeve, grabs R shoulder, pulling into hug. Open guard → R leg bites to hold OP down.
  • L foot plants to rotate 45°. Hip up to get L leg over to R side of head trapping arm. Both legs bite and squeeze.
  • If OP tries to stack: break dance back, spin 180° maintaining squeeze and tension on arm.
  • Armbar from stand-up guard pass: feet on hips, lock wrists, L leg to R hip, hip up, L leg over head, R leg into armpit, squeeze → close triangle (foot points away from OP arm).

Closed Guard Roll to Mount — Mitch

  • Cross sleeve grip then fig 4. Pull across body while using legs to break posture.
  • L arm traps R tricep, reaches back to OP L lat. R arm hooks OP L leg — OP pressed against you, R arm cross-trapped.
  • Use legs under hook to spin and stack OP above you. Pendulum roll OP to left. Catch R underhook during roll → land in mount.
  • If OP escapes R arm while stacked: press and square up again. Shove L elbow across body → set up triangle. Hip up to air, keep OP arm across thigh, close triangle.

Guard to Helicopter Armbar / Mount — Dan

  • From guard: break posture, pull forward with legs. Grab far lat with L arm. Unlock guard, L foot push off OP R hip.
  • R arm to OP L leg to spin to 45°→90°. If you can trap R arm, do so.
  • Helicopter onto your shoulder blades and stack OP on you. Squeeze legs together and roll to their back for mount — or armbar if R arm captured.

Pulling Guard — Dan (10/31)

  • Lapel grip to same side foot to hip. Pull forward off balance to lift OP in air. As they come down lock guard.
  • Move into attack quickly — get legs in, stand up. Triangle if one arm in, helicopter rotate, cross collar choke.
  • Pull guard defense: get low, wide squat base, dig elbows in. Don't let arm in (triangle) — just elbows to frame knees.

De La Riva to Weight Imbalance — Dan

  • De La Riva to weight imbalance → roll over head → mount.
  • OR roll, suspend, turn OP 40° → armbar.

Greg Rolling Notes — Guard Push/Pull

  • In guard: push/pull balance — sleeve collar with feet on hips or shoulder. Allow pull into guard; prevent pass.
  • Force a decision from mount: forearm into neck → OP brings elbow in → upside-down triangle, OR elicit arm extension → armbar.

Triangle & Straight Arm Bar (from guard) — Darrel Private

Lasso Guard — Breaking & Working (3/6/26)

💡
Key Note
Lasso is not a long-term guard — hard on knees and fingers. Use it as a triangle setup vehicle, not a sustained position.
  • Breaking lasso: They anchor lasso-side elbow to floor → rotate them to opposite side to lift it → tuck fist/wrist behind their arm and leg to break grip → simultaneously knee slice opposite side → straight into pass
  • Sweep: Remove foot from biceps → pass it over to hip on lasso side → raise leg like X-guard → execute pass
  • Triangle 1: Spin on back into triangle
  • Triangle 2: Remove lasso, go outside, shoot triangle
  • Triangle 3: Release lasso, grab arm behind biceps, pull into triangle
  • Butterfly-spider triangle: Both sleeves, knees flared into arms (no feet up) → pull straight into triangle. Works from spider or lasso.

Bread Cutter (Gi) — 6/23

💡
Key mechanic
Ulnar wrist perpendicular to throat — radial deviation pushes ulna into throat. Sprawl out to finish.
  • From side mount (R knee blocks R hip). L arm across straight down back from OP L shoulder. R arm block at OP R hip.
  • Start waddling legs up toward head. R arm down — underhook OP L armpit. Keep turning legs to head.
  • ★ "Smear" OP trapped R arm until released under R arm.
  • Grab inside R lapel with R hand. Swing legs back to side. Grab/trap OP R arm at elbow/bicep.
  • Control/grab OP L hand to trap against his chest (or let go over head).
  • If trapped: head pins L arm while R hand thumb inside L lapel. Wrist over neck.
  • Sprawl legs out — perpendicular. Ulnar R wrist perpendicular to throat. Radial deviation pushes ulna into throat.

4 Arm Attacks from Side Mount — 8/25

  • 1. Figure 4: R hand open palm to OP L wrist, motorcycle throttle. Scoot north, distance closed. Dive L arm under tricep for figure 4. ★ Roll up onto OP R side, knees to back to hip. Jam down / pull up to break lock. Internally rotate OP L arm behind back → submit.
  • 2. Collar Grab Shoulder Trap: OP L arm hugs, R arm has underhook. Reach to own collar or shoulder (No-Gi). Tuck elbow tight — control shoulder. Waddle knees north. Sit on head if needed. Rotate to OP other side, turn them on their side. Squeeze knees → armbar.
  • 3. Spiral Arm Bar: R hand to OP L tricep. Trap arm between belly and thigh in inguinal fold. Swing R thigh over belt — L knee to R hip. Lean R and L legs over head. Lean L to clear L foot. Squeeze knees → armbar.
  • 4. Swimming Straight Arm Attack: Swim L arm under OP L arm. Reach back for OP L shoulder. Swing legs north. Squeeze knees → armbar.

♥ Choke Defense — Protect Your Neck (from Back)

❤️
Favorite — marked in notes
All head movement is toward the direction their choke hand is pointing.
  • When OP has your back and hooks in: get hand control of their thumb and peel away.
  • Pass their choke hand to L hand — always keep free hand protecting the neck.
  • Move head — all movement toward the direction their choke hand is pointing — for head position.

Sequence & Chain Reference (KK Doc — Part 3)

  • X-Guard → Side Control → Chair Sit → Back: Follow close after X-guard. Throw leg over, stay close with pressure, straight to chair sit. Or into side control → can opener space → chair sit → back.
  • Push-Pull Ankle Pick → X-Guard Ankle Attack: Can always transition the push-pull ankle pick to X-guard ankle attack.
  • Guard Pull Leg Sweep → "Down the Tree": Leg sweep from guard pull can transition to chopping down the tree — wrap leg in popliteal fossa and slide backward.
  • Guard Pull → Triangle Chain: Foot on hip same-side sleeve → extend → other foot to bicep → bilateral count roll → shoot triangle → complete or other leg over face → pull hooked leg for angle → roll back to AB.
  • Side Control → Back: Can opener space → maintain pressure/contact → chair sit → back.
  • Kimura → Torrico-Plata (Lapel Gripped): OP has grabbed own lapel to block kimura → release kimura grip → grab thigh with R hand → L hand pushes head down → take back.
  • James Choke (Gift Wrap): Gift wrap → other arm into top side underhook → fist moves SCM out of the way → release gift wrap → grab own wrist → James choke.
  • Butterfly Elevation → Back (full): Two-on-one wrist and behind elbow → push into chest → elevate and push arm → initiate spin while dropping leg → get other hook → RNC.
  • Chair Sit → Back (full): From guard pass → chair sit (knee against head, kimura grip or wrist control) → chest to back → push through → second hook → RNC.
  • Takedown → Rapid Guard Sweep: Takedown attack sequences transition immediately to rapid guard sweep sequences — best way to avoid being taken down is to immediately attack after.
  • Front Headlock Bait Chain: Attack with intent → OP pulls back → front headlock. OR: anytime OP offers head → single-arm front headlock → cup chin + underhook simultaneously → flip to back → pull into lap → fall backward.
  • Armdrag Lineman → Valley Drop
  • Guard Pass Attack Tree: Zero contact → full engagement → pin shoulder → knee slice one side → if resistance, switch sides. Take what is offered: armbar (underhook), collar choke, Americana, Kimura → if resisted → Torricoplata.
  • Fake to Head → Leg Drag Chain: Fake to head → go for leg drag. Or: leg drag → knee slice.
  • ★ DON'T Cross Your Body: Crossing arms across body allows OP to take your back and climb up your lat. When someone else crosses their body — take their back.
  • HAVE A PLAN: Not rigid but have one or two go-to moves. Know what you're trying to do and have a 1:2 bait-and-switch. Clock for timing — look for openings. Every attack creates a defensive response that opens something else.
  • Trevor's Continuous Attack Principle: Continuous series of attacks forces OP to eventually open up. No pause — attack one after another in a way that doesn't expose you. Every attack feeds the next.

Head/face pins arm; elbow into neck. If they straighten → straight armbar opposite thumb.

🌟 Kevin's Bread Cutter

R hand L hip → L arm over/down blocking hip wall → smear arm → force arm pop out → R knee block → face pins arm → thumb to lapel.

🌟 Kimura from Side Control — Finish Options

If OP straightens arm: convert to straight armbar. If OP bends arm: smear L hand under tricep → motorcycle finish.

🌟 Kimura Grip Anatomy

Your hand controls their opposite wrist; other hand enters back of their armpit to grab your own wrist. Always sets up from inside wrist control. ★ Guillotine — Armpit Cue Armpit over back of head/occiput — control head & break posture. Curl into fetal/ball-line — NOT extending to rip head off. Close the V: rock the baby, prayer-handed, or hybrid.

🌟 Baseball Choke Timing

Sell the frame (push away); L thumb sneaks in high as they pass; bat grip along back collar as they mount. ★ Leg Ankle Lock Mechanics Down foot locked on hip/iliac crest; up foot hooks non-locked inner thigh; squeeze knees; roll to leg-lock side; elbow back & turn — chest to floor. DO NOT extend back.

🌟 Triangle — When OP Turns Away

Get L foot on L hip & squeeze knees like armbar → get perpendicular reaching for leg they lock in.

🌟 Triangle Rule — You Have Someone in Guard

Anytime their arm is between your legs → set up triangle.

🌟 Triangle Danger — You Are in Someone's Guard

Never allow one or two arms between their legs — you'll get triangled. Never stick both arms up the middle — they will double arm bar you.

🌟 Omoplata Setup from Foot-on-Hip

Foot on hip with sleeve/collar; other foot to bicep. Sleeve arm bends at elbow pointing at your face — bend it using your thigh. Same leg goes over their arm while rocking forward to base.

🌟 Triangle/AB Shoot from Guard

Pull sleeve to straighten arm; other foot to bicep; off-balance OP forward; shoot triangle — hips off ground, pull arm across body. Complete triangle OR other leg over face, pull hooked leg for angle → roll back to AB.

🌟 Grip Breaking for Armbar — Nevin's 3 Options

1) Jajo double deep/low. 2) Deep elbow at AB elbow + other at wrist. 3) Lock own bones + slide L deep elbow to wrist to pry.

🌟 Armbar Spin from Open Guard

Upper arm to head/occiput; roll them; upper & lower arm across; turn to side; stab over their thigh leg.

🌟 Wrist Locks Are About Controlling the Elbow

Wrist locks are about controlling the elbow, not just the wrist.

🌟 Mickey's Underhook Takedown

L collar pull → R arm underhook → shuck → single leg → counter-clockwise spin → follow to ground → knee on belly or side control.

🌟 Single Leg Head Position

Single leg: wrap from the outside & head goes outside.

🌟 Dan Private — Single Leg Counter

Hook that foot behind their thigh; throw their head to ground — 'kick-start motorcycle.'

🌟 Dan Private — Double/Single Leg Defense

Keep wide enough base to prevent double leg. If they go for double or single → 'lego hands' to shoulders/neck & sprawl.

🌟 Dan Private — Front Headlock from Offered Head

Wrap single-arm front headlock → cup chin; other hand gets underhook simultaneously; flip to back & pull into lap; fall back → get mount or side control.

🌟 Dan Private — Inside Horse Collar Escape

Go outside; closed fist to neck or face — forcefully extend to tear them away. If you catch their arm: pull into extension; turn hip into armpit; other hand to tricep (all while turning) → bring to ground.

🌟 Collar Pull + Ankle Pick Takedown

Collar grip + pull forward; if they resist → ankle pick; neck up for their down; pull back & immediately go after.

🌟 Torreano Defense / Collar Drag

Anytime someone has both hands on both your legs/knees or Kimura → collar grip & down → batting side → pull Torreano while battling.

🌟 Seated Takedown Drill

Roll up to wrap arm outside leg/ankles → 180° turn → throw palm to inner thigh → other hand collects knee → subtract space with legs while driving forward with head.

🌟 Punch Defense & Side Hump Throw

Hands in front → stomp, cover face into chest → slide L hand to OP R tricep → underhook R arm to OP L armpit → step in front → bunny hop feet together → butt peels to side → throw. If they step out: sweep their leg from behind; close space to ground.

🌟 Upright Headlock Escape

Look up → underhook armpit → push chin → grab R leg → pelvis lift → drop.

🌟 Bent-Over Headlock Escape

Bury face → R arm shoots across → L arm pulls bicep back → R arm controls wrist to back → separate or RNC.

🌟 Never Let Hands Past Knees in Guard

Never let their hands past your knees. Two grips → knees inside their antecub & butterfly open.

🌟 Closed Guard Elbow/Hand Position

Elbows in, hands on pelvis extended — always recover holsters.

🌟 X-Guard Shoulder Control

In X-guard: control shoulder forward; grab wrist as soon as possible.

🌟 Facing Away When Getting Passed

Getting passed means you end up facing away. Prevention: turtle & holster; elbows inside; knees inside; tuck arms between hips while holstering — they always go to the outside.

🌟 Shoulder Rule — Top & Bottom

Get OP shoulders to mat; never let your own shoulder get flat to mat.

🌟 Space Rule — Top vs. Bottom

On bottom: create space. On top: take away space. ★ Everything Happens at 90° & 45° Never be laying flat on your back. If briefly on your back: immediately frame — don't rest — create space & hip out.

🌟 Control with Balance of Push/Pull

You obtain control with balance of push/pull. Don't lead with arms — elbows in, protect.

🌟 Controlling the Head

Controlling the head prevents movements of the torso & shoulders.

🌟 Joint Submission Rule

For any joint submission: control joints above & below. For shoulder: it's elbow & head.

🌟 You Can't Take What You Want

You can't take what you want — you have to take what they give you. ★ Always Protect Your Neck Top priority at all times.

🌟 Metronome

Constantly be moving to the next place.

🌟 Always Moving Between Forms

Always moving between forms — butterfly / turtle / combat base — always connected.

🌟 In Turtle — Space Between Elbows & Hips

Closing space between elbows & hips is as important as protecting neck. Maintain wide base.

🌟 Standing Posture

Don't stick your neck out. Keep everything centered; maintain stance & balance; solid feet; elevate up & down like a squat. ★ HQ Pearl — Stable Base Stable base, wide, low center of gravity. The mat is long — don't allow the sweep.

🌟 Strategic Patience

When trying to escape mount or side control — wait until they do something.

🌟 Trevor's Continuous Attack Principle

Going through a continuous series of attacks forces OP to eventually open up. No pause — attack one after another in a way that doesn't obviously expose you.

🌟 Sandwich Grip

Extending elbow using sandwich grip — either to control elbow or make space.

🌟 Hand Control / Wrist Bend

Bend wrist while rotating to grab meat of thenar — apply pressure.

🌟 Black Belt Rolling Map (Gary & Ray)

Know your attack and escape options from every position — mount, side control, guard, inside guard, inside mount. Position-by-position, always have a plan.

From the Handwritten Journal — Dedicated Pearl Pages

🌟 Don't Shelf Both Arms Up the Middle

When you have someone in guard and their arm is between your legs — set up the primary. They will double-cross their legs. Never allow one arm between someone's legs or you will get triangled.

🌟 When Someone Has Your Back

Turn them away from you. Use arm/forearm against their hip. Set up the mount escape more than passing if they're particularly big. Always turn them toward your stronger side.

🌟 Always Protect Your Neck First

Before anything else. Controlling the head prompts movement of the torso and shoulders. Prevent side elbow connection — constantly shuffle both elbows away from their grips.

🌟 Use Arm/Forearm Against the Hip

When trying to pass or when someone is particularly big — frame against the hip with your forearm. Set up mount more than passing when they're heavy. This buys space.

🌟 Nothing Happening at 90° & 45°

If nothing is happening when you're flat on your back — fly on your feet. Get up. Don't be a rug. Movement creates opportunity; stillness gives them time to settle.

🌟 When Someone Has You In Guard

Anytime their arm is before you — set up primary, they will double-cross their legs. Don't allow one arm between someone's legs. 2 hands choking someone in guard = they will double-cross. Always set up primary first.

🌟 Controlling the Head Controls Everything

For any joint submission — controlling the head is what prompts the torso to move. You can't submit someone if you let them have what they give you. Always controlling head = always controlling where the shoulder/elbow goes.

🌟 Preventing Side Elbow — Constantly Shuffle

The side elbow connection is what kills you in guard. Constantly be shuffling elbows away from their body — never let them settle against your hips. It's ongoing, not a one-time fix.

🌟 Always Be Aware of Balance (Fears page)

When someone has a cross-face on you — your hips are what matter. Your hips is what's pressing down + flattening them. In the close, your hips is enormously important. Center of gravity + balance = everything.

🌟 Get Opponent's Shoulders to the Mat

Whatever position you're in — getting OP shoulders to the mat is the goal. Wrist control is about controlling the elbow. Elbow control is about controlling the shoulder. Shoulder to mat = you win the frame war.

🌟 When Passing Guard — You Are Rolling

Guard passing isn't static. You are constantly rolling, adjusting, flowing. Most reliable guard passes come out of continuous movement — not from one committed static attack. Follow what they give you.

🌟 When Someone Is Strangling You (Choke Defense)

Get your hand in between BOTH of them (thumb up in the middle). 2 hands choking you in guard = they will double-cross their legs. Use that moment — turn it into your escape.

🌟 Milney Armbar: Guard to Back Roll

Success across body OP before bellowing off. Cross body before you go. The guard-to-back rolling armbar is always there — you just need to cross their body first before the roll.

🌟 Controlling the Joint Controls the Submission

For any submission — controlling the head prompts movements of the torso and shoulders. For shoulder control — only allow elbow below. You cannot submit someone if you let them have any part of the frame back.

FUNDAMENTALS
Universal principles that apply from every position — the bedrock of your game
📌
How To Use This Section
These aren't position-specific techniques — they're the principles underneath everything. When something goes wrong in a roll, the answer is almost always here. Review before training. Add to it constantly.

Mindset & Movement Quality

🐢 Slow Down

Speed is the enemy of technique at your stage. Slow is smooth, smooth is controlled, controlled is dangerous. When you feel yourself spinning up — pause. Reset. Breathe. The roll will still be there.

🦶 No Happy Feet

Feet moving without purpose = loading up on the skids. Every step should have an intention. Planted base. Lead with knees not feet. If your feet are near their hands, you're already in trouble.

🎯 Intentionality

No happy movement — every movement has a purpose. Know what you're trying to accomplish before you move. Aimless movement burns energy and creates openings. Have a plan before you execute.

✂️ Clean & Precise

Attention to detail in every movement. Sloppy technique at slow speed becomes sloppy technique at fast speed. Do it right slowly first. The mechanics matter more than the speed.

Posture & Base

🏛️ Head Position

Always know where your head is. Head up = good posture. Head down = broken posture = you're about to get submitted or swept. Your head leads your spine. Whoever wins the head battle usually wins the position.

⚓ Stable Base

Wide, low center of gravity. The mat is long — don't allow the sweep. Solid planted feet. Square stance. When passing guard: get your ass DOWN, lead with knees. A high base is an unstable base.

🚫 No Extended Arms

Any extended arm is an armbar invitation. Elbows stay in, close to your body. When posting, posting to defend — get them back in fast. Extended arms lose structural integrity the moment they straighten.

🚫 Don't Enter Inside the Legs

Both arms inside their legs = triangle. One arm inside carelessly = triangle. Never stick both arms up the middle. Stay on the outside. If you must enter — do it decisively with a plan.

Top Game Principles

📌 Tailbone DOWN When Passing

When passing guard — tailbone on the floor of your opponent. Heavy hips. Low center of gravity. This pins them, kills their hip mobility, and makes your base immovable. High hips = they escape.

🔒 Take Space Away

On top: eliminate all space. Space is what they use to escape, frame, and recover guard. The moment you pass — close the distance, pin the near shoulder, take away every inch. They need space to move. You need to remove it.

💪 Pin Shoulders First

When passing guard or achieving a dominant position — pin both shoulders simultaneously in one quick decisive move. Not sequential. One move. Then establish your base. Sequential shoulder control gives them time to frame and escape.

🔗 Elbow-Knee Connection

The spine of top game defense. When passing: elbow stomps to knee as you step in. In over-under: L elbow TIGHTLY connected to L knee — or you get triangled. This connection closes the triangle danger and controls their hip mobility.

Bottom Game Principles

🚨 Never Flat on Your Back

This is the cardinal rule of the bottom game. Flat on your back = they control you completely. Always on your side, always moving, always framing. The moment you go flat — get back on your side immediately. This applies everywhere: guard retention, escapes, after being taken down.

📌 Tailbone UP When Retaining Guard

Opposite of passing — when retaining guard your tailbone should be OFF the floor. Hips up and active. Active hips create frames, enable shrimping, and make it impossible for them to settle into a pass. Dead hips = passed guard.

🏃 You Can Always Escape & Stand

When taken down or guard passed — remember you always have the option to escape and return to standing. Don't get trapped in a mental state of "I must submit from here." Getting back to your feet is a completely valid and often smart option.

🛡 Frames Create Space

Frames are structural — elbow to knee, knee to hip, forearm across chest. They create distance and buy time. Without frames you're just hoping they don't settle. Frames are your first line of defense from any bottom position.

Grips & Control

✋ Deny Their Grips

Grips are the entry point for everything. If they can't establish grips — they can't execute techniques. Push away at the neck. Strip wrist grips immediately. Two-on-one to break grips. Don't let them settle into collar and sleeve control.

🎯 Control Their Head

Controlling the head prevents movement of the torso and shoulders. Whoever wins the head battle controls the position. In top game: cross-face. In front headlock: head wrap. In guard passing: control posture to control head.

⇄ Push-Pull Always

Every effective technique uses simultaneous push-pull. Pushing one part while pulling another creates angular force they cannot resist with straight-line strength. Never push alone. Never pull alone. Always both.

🦵 Keep Them Inside Your Legs

In guard — they should always be inside your legs and feet. The moment they get outside your legs the guard is gone. Frame with your feet on hips, shins on biceps, or knees as barriers. Your legs are your first defense.

🔍 Von Flue Choke — Guillotine Counter

Got tapped by this today. Had a loose guillotine on someone, they trapped my choking arm, applied shoulder pressure, got a choke. That's the Von Flue Choke — named after UFC fighter Jason Von Flue who submitted Alex Karalexis at UFC Fight Night 3 (2006).

How it works:
  • You have a guillotine attempt but it's not tight
  • Opponent passes to side control on the OPPOSITE side of your guillotine arm (the "weak side")
  • They pin your choking arm against your neck using their head and shoulder — you can't let go now
  • They gable grip their hands, locking the arm in place
  • Drive the near shoulder firmly into the side of your neck — compresses the carotid
  • Your own trapped arm compresses the other side — two-sided pressure = blood choke
  • Tripod the legs to increase downward pressure through the shoulder
Why it caught you: Loose guillotine with arm draped over. Once they trap the arm and pass to side control, you're locked — can't release, can't escape without defending the pass first.

How to prevent it:
  • Finish the guillotine immediately — don't hold a loose one
  • High elbow guillotine (Marcelotine) is harder to Von Flue because the elbow is elevated over their shoulder
  • If they start passing — transition immediately, don't keep the loose grip
  • If caught: frame against their shoulder/neck to relieve pressure, tap with feet if needed
🎯
The Lesson
A loose guillotine isn't just ineffective — it's a liability. Finish it fast or let it go. The Von Flue is the punishment for holding a bad guillotine.
